III.   The Gap That Remains

Every information processing system — every model, every algorithm, every automated decision pipeline — operates on a domain. A domain is the set of conditions under which the system's outputs are valid.

The Problem

That domain is almost never formally declared. It is embedded in training data, in spreadsheet formulas, in the judgment of the engineer who built the system — but it is not written down in a mathematically examinable form before processing begins.

The consequences are not speculative. They are measured in dollars, and the measurement is precise.

V.2   Domain Closure as a Stability Requirement

The preceding operator descriptions specify what the framework computes. This section states why that computation produces stability — and why its absence permits instability even in systems that appear to function correctly.

A system may accept valid inputs and produce correct outputs on every observed pass, and still exhibit unbounded or unstable behavior over time. The reason is structural: the system does not enforce the condition

\[ T(D) \;\subseteq\; D \]

where \( D \) is the declared operating domain and \( T \) is the system's transformation. Domain closure — the requirement that \( T \) map every element of \( D \) back into \( D \) — is the mathematical condition that separates bounded evolution from eventual divergence. Without it, each pass through the system is permitted to drift incrementally beyond the domain in which its logic was validated. No individual output need be visibly wrong. The accumulation is what produces the failure.

Most operational systems do not explicitly declare \( D \). The domain is implicit in training data, in engineering tolerances, in the professional judgment of the team that built the system. Because \( D \) is undeclared, closure under \( T \) cannot be verified before execution. The standard mitigations — monitoring, threshold alerts, periodic revalidation, post hoc correction — all operate after the domain boundary has already been crossed. The detection gap documented in Section IV is a direct consequence of this sequence: a boundary that has not been declared cannot be approached detectably.

The ABRCE composition enforces domain closure by construction. The Admissibility operator \( A \) declares \( D \). The Bounded Stabilization operator \( C \) constrains the output of the relational transformation to remain within \( D \). The composition
